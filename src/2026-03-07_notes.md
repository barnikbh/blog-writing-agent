This week I wanted to understand how modern cars are assembled in factories.

At a high level, car manufacturing is a pipeline where raw materials gradually become a finished vehicle. The process is highly automated and divided into specialized stages.

The main stages of car manufacturing are:

1. Stamping
2. Body assembly
3. Painting
4. Powertrain installation
5. Final assembly
6. Quality testing

Each stage has its own machines, robots, and quality checks.

Stamping is where large sheets of steel or aluminum are pressed into body parts such as doors, roofs, and hoods using massive stamping presses.

Body assembly is where robotic arms weld these parts together to form the car's structural shell, often called the "body-in-white".

Painting is a multi-step process. First the body is cleaned, then coated with primer, then painted, and finally sealed with a protective clear coat.

Powertrain installation is where the engine, transmission, and drivetrain components are installed into the body.

Final assembly includes installing interiors, electronics, seats, dashboards, wheels, and glass.

After that the car goes through a series of quality tests including:

- electrical testing
- brake testing
- leak detection
- road simulation

What I found interesting is that modern factories operate like a continuous production pipeline where each station performs a specific task before passing the car to the next stage.

In a way, the system is similar to a software pipeline where each module performs a transformation step.
